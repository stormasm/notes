### Royalty Purple Pod Bush Beans

- [royalty purple pod seeds](https://trueleafmarket.com/products/bean-bush-royal-burgundy)

Royalty Purple Pod bush beans typically contain approximately 80 to 100 seeds per ounce. For planting, one ounce is usually sufficient to sow a short row, while a full pound is recommended for larger, more productive garden plots, commonly available at suppliers like True Leaf Market.

Average Seed Count: ~80-100 seeds/oz.

Specific Range: 84 to 95 seeds per ounce are frequently cited in Everwilde Farms and Victory Seed Company listings.

Weight Metric: Approximately 1,300–1,500 seeds per pound.


These heirloom, stringless beans are known for their dark purple pods, which turn green upon cooking, and their ability to germinate well in cold, wet soil.

Related Varieties
Be aware that similar purple bush beans, such as the Royal Burgundy, often have a similar count of roughly 87–110 seeds per ounce.

Yes, Royalty Purple Pod bush beans are excellent to eat; they are specifically classified as snap beans, meaning the entire pod and the immature seeds inside are meant to be consumed together.

The pods are stringless, crisp, and tender with a delicious, sweet flavor that is enjoyable both raw in salads and cooked.

While the pods are bright purple when raw, they turn green during cooking due to the breakdown of anthocyanins, though the taste remains high quality.

They are highly productive and easy to harvest, with the contrasting purple color making them easy to spot on the bush compared to traditional green varieties.

### Georgia Southern Collard Greens


- [all collard green seeds](https://trueleafmarket.com/collections/collard-garden-seed)
- [collards georgia southern](https://trueleafmarket.com/products/collards-georgia-southern)

Yes, Georgia Southern collard greens (a classic,, heat-tolerant, and cold-hardy heirloom) grow very well in Oregon. Because they thrive in cooler weather and can handle frost, they are ideal for Oregon’s spring and, particularly, fall/winter gardens.


Key Tips for Success in Oregon:
Best Planting Times: For a fall/winter harvest, plant seeds in mid-to-late summer (about 10 weeks before the first frost). For spring, start indoors 6–8 weeks before the last frost, or direct sow in late April through mid-July.
Soil: They prefer well-drained soil rich in organic matter (amend with compost).
Light: They grow best in full sun but can tolerate partial shade.
Water & Care: Keep soil consistently moist, providing 1–1.5 inches of water per week.
Harvesting: You can start harvesting leaves 60–75 days after planting, with the best flavor often appearing after the first frost.

### Golden Wax Bush Beans

- [ref seed](https://trueleafmarket.com/products/bean-wax-bush-top-notch-golden-wax)

Yes, Golden Wax bush beans grow exceptionally well in the Willamette Valley, producing high yields of crisp, yellow pods. They thrive in our warm summers, with OSU Extension Service recommending planting from late April through July, similar to other bush beans. They are reliable, quick to produce (45-60 days), and prefer sunny spots with well-drained soil, making them a great choice for local gardeners.

Tips for Success in the Willamette Valley:
Planting Timing: Direct sow seeds directly in the ground, says the Oregon State University extension service, when the soil has warmed to at least 60°F, typically between the end of April and late June, according to the Oregon State University's publication on beans.
Soil Preparation: Ensure the soil is well-drained and prepare it with a bit of organic matter to improve yields.
Spacing & Depth: Plant seeds 1 inch deep and 4–6 inches apart to ensure good air circulation.
Care & Harvesting: Water at the base to keep foliage dry, as mentioned on Permies.com. Harvest regularly when pods are young and tender, generally around the same time as you would a green bean.
